Ludum Dare 36: SPATCON Postmortem Play SPATCON here!

Since summer was going to end soon, I figured, what better way to spend a nice, sunny weekend than developing games? In this post, Iâ€™ll briefly discuss my experience with the 36th Ludum Dare game jam.

Challenge: Lack Of A Unified Vision

The theme for the August jam was Ancient technology. The team decided early on that we would be making a rhythm game that would feature ancient instruments. Although we initially agreed upon the gameplay, we ended up going with a different design halfway through. This caused a lot of the earlier code to be scrapped. I felt that this was caused by the lack of a unified vision within the team. Furthermore, development was occasionally interrupted so that the team could discuss whenever a critical decision was to be made.

Resolution: Hourly stand-ups

We decided to hold hourly stand-ups to update the team with our individual progress. This proved incredibly useful as the standups allowed the team to refine the end goals of the project as we diver deeper into development. It essentially allowed us to flexibly develop the game to a constantly-changing vision and design.

Considering that we only had the weekend, I did not think my experience with SCRUM would have been relevant. Yet, the agile methodology allowed us to produce a well-polished game in such a short time.

Victory: Team Ownership

With that said, the shared ownership of the team was wonderful. Everyone had a say in every decision the team made. We were able to voice out our opinions as the team critiqued ideas and not people. It enforced the spirit of collaboration and made the project uniquely ours.

Challenge: Technical ability

The biggest challenge I faced during the game jam was my technical ability. Since I was a student who was trying to keep up with professionals, I felt like I should not have been part of the team. My capabilities paled in comparison to the technical prowess of the indie veterans that were my teammates. I felt that I was holding the team back and felt bad for it.

Resolution: Mentorship

Despite the weakness of my technical abilities, the team was very supportive of me. Every question I asked was met with answers, suggestions, and explanations. For example, I was taught some of the mechanics of midi files and how we could implement the timing for each beat.

Victory: Initiative

The guilt pushed me to work a lot harder than I expected. I did every little thing I could do to contribute, no matter how minor. I took the liberty of scrapping deprecated code and tidying up the code base. I tried to ensure that the game was usability-oriented by repeatedly applying heuristic evaluations. I also assisted in the discovery and resolution of bugs and defects throughout the weekend. Finally, I took charge of creating our Ludum Dare submission entry which took a lot more creativity than I initially expected. Although my contributions to the game have been relatively minor and slow, I think I earned my place in the team by the end of it.
